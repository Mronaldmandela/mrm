Research

Working Papers

Labor Market Outcomes in South-Africa

Abstract:Poor health can limit economic opportunity, especially in settings of high unemployment. Yet in low-income countries, we know little about how health shapes labor market outcomes over time, or how these effects vary by demographics, or policy contexts like Child Support Grant (CSG)–a large income support program in South Africa. I study this using nationally representative panel data from South Africa and estimate dynamic models of hours worked and labor force participation (LFP). I find that health stock improves both outcomes, but the gains are unequally distributed: Women face steep penalties in the labor market, only partially offset by health stock; Whites benefit most on hours, Asians on LFP, while Coloured individuals respond only on the extensive margin. I also test how health interacts with the CSG, and find that it generally raises the chance of LFP, but its effects vary across subgroups. These results show that health plays a key but uneven role in shaping labor outcomes, and that integrating health investments with targeted income support may be more effective than pursuing either alone.

Parenthood, Locus of Control, and the Impact of Voluntary Health Policy on Dietary Outcomes

With Christine Eckert, Mehdi Hosseinkouchack, Natalina Zlatevskac

Abstract:Can voluntary nutrition policies improve dietary quality, particularly among parents with differing personal control beliefs? This study evaluates the impact of Australia’s 2014 voluntary Health Star Rating (HSR) policy on healthy eating, focusing on the moderating roles of parenthood and locus of control (LoC). Using five waves of HILDA data (2007–2021), we construct comprehensive Healthy Eating Index (HEI) measures and apply a fixed-effects difference-in-differences design, stratified by LoC tertiles to assess treatment heterogeneity. The treatment (unhealthy eaters) group shows heterogeneous post-2014 improvements, with the largest gains among parents with lower LoC (e.g., a 4.4% relative increase among externally oriented individuals). Our findings suggest that voluntary information policies can shift consumption patterns when they resonate with household contexts such as parental responsibilities. Effectiveness, however, depends on psychological orientation: externally oriented individuals benefit most from external guidance mechanisms. These results show the potential of information-based policies to influence health behaviors and highlight the critical role of psychological factors and parental responsibilities in policy success.
